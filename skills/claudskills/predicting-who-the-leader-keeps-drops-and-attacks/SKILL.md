# Predicting Who the Leader Keeps, Drops, and Attacks

> *What this skill is about, in one sentence:*
> How a dominance-seeking leader decides — not morally but operationally — which people to
> retain, which to rehabilitate after conflict, which to discard as losers, and which to attack,
> with constituency (not loyalty) as the master variable. And what that decision logic means
> for anyone in the orbit trying to preserve their position.

## Where this comes from

This skill distills Chapter 7 ("Trump's World of Winners and Losers") of Sonnenfeld & Tian's
*Trump's Ten Commandments* (2025), with material from Chapter 5 (fluidity of relationships) and
Chapter 1 (loyalty-as-one-way).

Academic and cultural foundations:

- **Niccolò Machiavelli**, *The Prince* (1513). The foundational text on power-based rather than
  loyalty-based retention logic. Not explicitly cited by Sonnenfeld but the ancestor of the
  framework.
- **Groucho Marx** — "I don't want to belong to any club that would have me as a member."
  Sonnenfeld cites this as the operative logic: if the only people still loyal to you are
  nobodies, you are radioactive.
- **Carl von Clausewitz**, *On War* (1832), and the later tradition of **Realpolitik** (Ludwig
  von Rochau, 1853) through Bismarck, Kissinger, Brzezinski. The core claim: alliances should
  be based on mutual strategic interest, not emotional bonds or shared values. Trump's
  relational style is "Realpolitik applied to personal relationships."
- **Walter Bagehot**, *The English Constitution* (1867). Quoted in Ch. 1: "No man can argue on
  his knees."
- **Jonathan Karl**, *Retribution* (2024). Cited in Ch. 7 for the Mike Pence interview on why
  Pence was startled by how quickly Trump turned against him.

Primary source: `false`. The operators describe a pattern of relational calculus that can fire
in any dominance-seeking leader; they are not tied to the specific person.

## North Star

> *Does this operator change what the user decides or does next?*

If an operator helps the user understand why the leader is cruel or volatile but doesn't change
whether they stay, exit, publicly criticize, rebuild constituency, or avoid being the next
target — cut it.

## Opening Gate: does this skill apply?

Three tests:

- **Sort is visibly happening.** The leader is actively retaining/discarding/attacking people
  around them in ways that surprise conventional loyalty-based observers.
- **Criteria are not explicit morality or loyalty.** People whose "loyalty" was greater than
  others have been dropped; people whose "betrayals" were greater have been rehabilitated.
- **Public constituency seems to matter.** Whether the person retains an audience,
  supporters, or glamour seems to drive the leader's treatment more than the person's behavior.

If all three flag, apply this skill. If only one, the situation may be ordinary
HR/political-appointee dynamics.

## The Operators

### 1. `winner-vs-loser-by-constituency-not-virtue`

**Plain English:** The master sort rule. A "winner" in this leader's universe is someone who
projects success, glamour, toughness, *and above all retains a constituency* — an audience that
affirms their status. A "loser" is not defined by crime, immorality, or ordinary failure, but by
loss of constituency. Who has ears still listening to them determines how the leader treats
them.

**Source:** Sonnenfeld & Tian, Ch. 7. The book's explicit claim: "A winner is someone who
projects success, glamour, toughness, and, above all, relevance. A winner has a following, an
audience, a crowd that affirms their status… A loser is not defined by crime, immorality, or
even failure in the traditional sense. Being a loser has more to do with status than any innate
reflection of self-worth."

**Detect:**
- The leader's retention patterns track constituency, not loyalty or merit.
- People with independent followings get more leeway; people entirely dependent on the leader
  for visibility get less.
- Critics are often left alone if they have loyal followings; loyalists are often discarded
  once their followings evaporate.

**Intent behind it:** Preserve the leader's own claim to winner status by association. Losers
contaminate; winners elevate.

**Counter-move:**
- **If you want to preserve your standing with this kind of leader, preserve your independent
  constituency.** Credentials, expertise, public visibility, audience loyalty. Constituency is
  not a side-effect of being useful — it is what makes you durably valuable.
- **If you lose your constituency, expect to be discarded regardless of past service.** Sarah
  Palin, not Sarah Palin's disloyalty, is the case (she did nothing wrong; her star faded and
  she was iced out).
- **Monitor your constituency in real time.** You are worth what your independent base thinks
  you are worth, not what you privately contributed.

**Do not use when:** the leader has demonstrated durable loyalty to people without constituency
over time. Rare, but possible — distinguishes dominance-seeking leaders from conventional
loyalty-based ones.

### 2. `rehabilitate-if-constituency-remains`

**Plain English:** Former critics and enemies can be rehabilitated — even extreme ones — if they
retain a constituency that the leader wants access to. The greater the prior criticism (and the
more cultural cachet the critic had), the stronger the signal of dominance when they convert.

**Source:** Sonnenfeld & Tian, Ch. 7. Cases: JD Vance (called Trump "America's Hitler," "noxious,"
"heroin" — rehabilitated into Senate endorsement and then VP). Marco Rubio ("Lil Marco,"
questioned Trump's manhood — rehabilitated into Secretary of State + National Security Advisor).
Lindsey Graham (originally called voting Trump "getting shot or taking poison"). "Lyin' Ted"
Cruz. Tim Burchett rehabilitated by simple Trump charm without policy concessions.

**Detect:**
- Former critic has a significant following, cultural cachet, or symbolic power.
- Former critic has visibly bent the knee (recanted, publicly supported, ceremonial loyalty
  display).
- Leader's rehabilitation of former critics is held up as demonstration of dominance — "look at
  who now works for me."

**Intent behind it:** Converting a loud critic is a pure show of strength. Nothing signals
dominance like "my loudest critic now serves me." The conversion is the trophy.

**Counter-move:**
- **If you are a former critic with constituency: you are in a strong position to negotiate.**
  Your prior criticism is not a liability; it is leverage. The leader wants the conversion
  trophy.
- **If you are a conventional ally watching a former critic rehabilitated above you:** your
  position is weaker than you think. The leader values conversions more than constant loyalty.
  Preserve your independent constituency so you stay in the "keep" category despite the lack
  of trophy value.
- **For observers predicting the leader's next alliance:** the most dramatic former critic with
  a constituency the leader wants will be rehabilitated next.

**Do not use when:** the former critic has lost their constituency. Then they are in the
discard category (operator 1), not the rehabilitation category.

### 3. `drop-invented-allies-keep-sovereign-ones`

**Plain English:** The leader feels most entitled to discard people they "invented" or
"discovered" — those who owe their entire public position to the leader. Allies with
independent power bases outside the relationship get much wider berth. This makes a concrete
prediction: people who can be framed as self-made-by-the-leader are disposable; people with
external legitimacy are not.

**Source:** Sonnenfeld & Tian, Ch. 7. Contrast: Elon Musk (too independent to be discarded, too
powerful to be embraced — treated as rival warlord, "American Prigozhin"); Bobby Kennedy
(independent constituency, wider berth despite heterodox views); compared to Tulsi Gabbard (the
leader's direct assessment per NYT: "Bobby's a star. Tulsi? Tulsi wants to be a star").

**Detect:**
- The person's public visibility/income/platform came largely through the leader.
- Alternative career paths are limited — the person would struggle to reach a comparable
  position without the leader.
- The leader publicly credits themselves for the person's position ("I made them").

**Intent behind it:** People who can be discarded without the leader losing anything valuable
are discarded. People who could hurt the leader on the way out are handled carefully.

**Counter-move:**
- **If you are being invented by the leader — take the opportunity but build parallel
  legitimacy.** Industry credentials, outside income streams, independent public profile.
  Invented allies are disposable; sovereign allies are not.
- **If you are sovereign — act it.** Leaders who sense independent power bases extend leeway.
  Acting dependent when you're not gives away leverage.
- **For predicting drops:** staff-level appointees whose credentials are weak independent of
  the leader will be dropped first when the leader needs to sacrifice someone.

**Do not use when:** the leader has demonstrated genuine commitment to invented allies over
time. Rare, but check.

### 4. `drown-the-squeaky-wheel-in-oil`

**Plain English:** The leader often prefers to *absorb* a critic — lavish attention, access,
small favors — rather than attack them or replace them. This works especially well on critics
who have a constituency the leader wants access to. It is often the first move on a new critic,
before attack is considered.

**Source:** Sonnenfeld & Tian, Ch. 5, Ch. 7 (and its most detailed version is in Sonnenfeld's
own account of his first encounter with Trump: Sonnenfeld wrote critical reviews of *The
Apprentice*; Trump offered him the presidency of Trump University, then golf). Cases: Tim
Burchett (charm-treatment converted him); Bill Maher (dinner softened him); Megyn Kelly (serial
hot-cold pattern); Mary Kissel (journalist-turned-advisor); Sarah Isgur (similar).

**Detect:**
- The leader's response to a new critic is not attack but access, attention, or small favors.
- The critic's subsequent coverage of the leader softens over time.
- Former critics who accepted the access often describe the leader as "charming" or
  "disarming" privately.

**Intent behind it:** Absorbing a critic is cheaper than fighting them — and often more
effective. Fighting amplifies; absorbing quiets.

**Counter-move:**
- **If you are a critic being offered access, understand the trade.** You gain information;
  you may gain real relationship. But the implicit price is softened future coverage. Decide
  consciously rather than drift.
- **Expect hot-cold pattern.** The leader who absorbs will also publicly attack when convenient.
  Megyn Kelly pattern: attack, absorb, attack, absorb. Personal animosity is not the driver;
  current utility is.
- **For observers predicting the leader's next move on a rising critic:** if the critic has
  constituency, oil before fire is the default.

**Do not use when:** the critic has crossed a line that makes absorption impossible (operator
6's territory). Then the move is retaliation, not absorption.

### 5. `selective-retribution`

**Plain English:** Contrary to public perception, a dominance-seeking leader's vindictive streak
is not indiscriminate — it is precisely calibrated. The leader pursues revenge only when they
are confident they have the upper hand: the target must be unsympathetic, isolated, and
unlikely to rally support. Sympathetic targets or targets with constituency are left alone.

**Source:** Sonnenfeld & Tian, Ch. 7. Book cases of selected targets: John Bolton (Republican-
turned-independent, no constituency), James Comey (appointed official, no elected constituency),
Chris Krebs (same), Val Demings quoted saying Democrats were relieved rather than alarmed about
Bolton indictment. Cases of non-targets: sitting Democratic elected officials are rarely
attacked with the same intensity because their constituency mobilizes for them.

**Detect:**
- The leader's retribution targets are typically isolated, politically homeless, or
  institutionally exposed.
- Targets are people whose own allies are unlikely to defend them (John Bolton pattern).
- When the leader *has* attacked someone with a constituency, the attack was less effective
  and often backfired.

**Intent behind it:** Make the attack's cost asymmetric — high for the target, low for the
leader. This requires picking targets who cannot mobilize defense.

**Counter-move:**
- **If you are potentially a target: build sympathetic constituency before you need it.**
  Political homelessness makes you cheap to attack. Even a modest constituency that would
  publicly defend you changes the calculus.
- **For observers predicting the next target:** the most isolated, least-sympathetic critic is
  next. Not the loudest.
- **If you are a target already and cannot mobilize defense, consider lowering profile.** High
  visibility without defenders is the worst position.

**Do not use when:** the leader has demonstrated willingness to attack sympathetic targets
repeatedly. Some do; the pattern is rarer but not absent.

### 6. `push-retribution-until-system-pushes-back`

**Plain English:** Retribution is pushed incrementally, testing how far the institutional system
will go along. When courts, prosecutors, or Senate stop the leader, they pull back. The push is
not commitment; it is probing. Understanding this produces specific predictions about what the
leader will and won't do.

**Source:** Sonnenfeld & Tian, Ch. 7. Book cases: Alina Habba as interim US Attorney in New
Jersey — pulled back when judicial refusal and unanimous appellate ruling stripped her of
authority. Lindsey Halligan in Eastern District of Virginia — high-profile cases unraveled on
procedural failure (unlawful appointment, grand jury refusal, barred evidence). Trump
"ultimately largely let his efforts to pursue legal retaliation lapse, abandoning his
appointments of loyalists as US attorneys and reverting back to the traditional blue-slip-Senate
confirmation process."

**Detect:**
- Retaliatory appointments/actions are pushed rapidly but withdrawn when courts or
  institutional bodies refuse.
- Pattern: test, push, retreat, test again. Not a do-or-die commitment at any single
  institutional boundary.
- The leader does not attack the institution itself frontally when it refuses — they test a
  different boundary next.

**Intent behind it:** Expand the field of what's operationally possible. The leader is not
committed to any single retaliatory outcome; they are committed to stretching the envelope.

**Counter-move:**
- **Institutional defenders: this is good news.** The institutional system *can* push back, and
  when it does, the leader retreats. The most important work is strengthening courts,
  Senate-confirmation norms, agency independence — the institutional boundaries themselves.
- **Specific cases: fight procedurally.** The Halligan and Habba cases were won on procedural
  grounds, not ideological ones. Judicial insistence on proper appointment, grand-jury
  independence, evidentiary rules.
- **Expect probing to continue.** A single successful pushback does not deter the pattern; it
  shifts it to a different boundary. Defenders need sustained institutional capacity, not
  one-time legal wins.

**Do not use when:** the system no longer pushes back — in which case the pattern changes from
probe-and-retreat to override. Watch for the transition.

### 7. `strike-adjacent-weakness`

**Plain English:** When the primary target is too strong to attack directly, the leader strikes
adjacent — at the target's aides, subordinates, spouses, employers, business partners. The goal
is achieved (pressure on the target) while the direct attack is avoided.

**Source:** Sonnenfeld & Tian, Ch. 7. Signature case: Trump's campaign against Fed Chair Jay
Powell. Powell personally was too strong to attack directly (GOP senators rushed to his defense).
Trump pivoted to attacking Fed Governor Lisa Cook on (potentially substantive) mortgage-fraud
allegations — striking adjacent to Powell via a more vulnerable adjacent target.

**Detect:**
- The leader's attack lands not on the primary adversary but on adjacent people with weaker
  institutional protection.
- The attack is framed on ostensibly substantive grounds that the target cannot easily refute.
- The primary adversary has de facto leverage reduced because their adjacent allies are
  consumed by self-defense.

**Intent behind it:** Force the primary adversary to either defend the adjacent target
(costly, distracting) or abandon them (reputational cost).

**Counter-move:**
- **For anyone adjacent to a likely primary target: assume you may be the attack vector.**
  Tighten your own institutional protections pre-emptively.
- **For primary targets: protect your adjacencies.** Aides, board members, business partners,
  family. The attack will come there first.
- **For institutional defenders: support the adjacent figures publicly.** Letting them be
  picked off individually weakens the whole chain; collective defense (see skill #4) applies.

**Do not use when:** the attack on the adjacent figure is genuinely merit-based and proceeds on
its own substantive grounds. Misreading a legitimate enforcement action as strike-adjacent
produces false outrage that undermines future warnings.

### 8. `preemptive-retaliation-via-asymmetric-leverage` (the Roffman case)

**Plain English:** When a critic surfaces publicly, a dominance-seeking leader often retaliates
not against the critic directly but against whoever holds leverage over the critic — their
employer, publisher, regulator. The asymmetric power (attacker's business leverage over the
employer vastly exceeds the critic's value to the employer) makes the critic disposable.

**Source:** Sonnenfeld & Tian, Ch. 7. Canonical case: Marvin Roffman, a research analyst at
Janney Montgomery, published research (correctly, as it turned out) that the Trump Taj Mahal
would be unable to cover its debts. Trump faxed the chairman of Janney Montgomery demanding
Roffman be fired and a public apology issued, threatening suit. The firm folded and terminated
Roffman. Later, after the Taj Mahal's actual bankruptcy confirmed Roffman's prediction, he won
a multimillion-dollar wrongful-termination settlement. But Trump's immediate goal (silencing the
critic) was achieved.

**Detect:**
- The critic is relatively junior and dependent on an institution for their platform.
- The institution has business or political interests the leader can influence.
- The leader's move is aimed not at the critic's arguments but at the institution's
  relationship with the critic.

**Intent behind it:** Achieve the silencing goal without the direct confrontation with the
critic. The institution does the work.

**Counter-move:**
- **For anyone whose criticism of a powerful leader exposes them via an employer: build direct
  audience reach.** Own media (newsletter, podcast, public platform) so that your employer is
  not the pressure point. Substack, independent research outlets, and direct subscriber models
  are partial defenses against this attack.
- **For institutions: pre-commit publicly to protecting analysts/journalists/employees from
  this pressure.** Public commitment increases the reputational cost of caving to it.
- **For observers predicting this move:** any junior critic making accurate predictions about
  the leader's weakness is a candidate. Watch the leverage chain, not the critic.

**Do not use when:** the institution's action against the critic is genuinely driven by the
critic's performance issues independent of the leader's pressure.
